### Key Features - Most important technical specifications
The most critical feature is the filter's certification. Look for NSF/ANSI certifications. NSF 42 certifies the reduction of aesthetic impurities like chlorine (taste and odor), while NSF 53 certifies the reduction of health-related contaminants like lead and mercury. This tells you how long you can expect it to perform effectively.

High-quality filters and pitchers are made from BPA-free, food-grade plastic. This ensures that the filter system itself doesn't leach any harmful chemicals into your water. Look for sturdy construction in pitchers and faucet mounts that can withstand daily use without cracking or breaking.

**Step 3: Prioritize NSF Certifications**
This is non-negotiable for ensuring performance. Look for NSF 42 for taste and odor, NSF 53 for health contaminants like lead, and NSF 372 for lead-free materials. A filter with these certifications has been independently verified to do what it claims.

**Step 4: Compare Lifespan and Cost-Per-Gallon**
Do not just look at the upfront price. A filter that lasts 200 gallons might cost more initially than one that lasts 40 gallons, but it will be much cheaper in the long run. Divide the cost of the filter by the number of gallons it treats to find the true cost-per-gallon.

**Step 5: Read Reviews and Check Compatibility**
If you are buying a replacement filter, double-check that it is compatible with your existing system. Read recent user reviews to gauge real-world performance, ease of installation, and any potential issues like reduced water flow.
